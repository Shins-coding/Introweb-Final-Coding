AMD GPU

AMD entered the GPU market prominently after acquiring ATI Technologies in 2006. The Radeon brand became known for graphics innovation, DirectX advancement, and strong game performance. Modern AMD GPUs leverage architectures like RDNA and Polaris, with RDNA 4 leading top performance, advanced ray tracing, and AI features. AMD competes with NVIDIA for both high-end gaming and value offerings, plus develops integrated graphics for mixed CPU-GPU APUs.

AMD GPU Series Overview

RX 9000 Series

The latest generation based on RDNA 4 architecture, delivering the highest performance, advanced ray tracing, and AI features. Targeted at premium 4K gaming, high-refresh 1440p gaming, and demanding content creation workloads. Key models like RX 9070 XT offer large VRAM and cutting-edge GPU tech.

RX 7000 Series

Previous generation built on RDNA 3, introducing improved efficiency, enhanced ray tracing compared to its predecessor, and large VRAM variants. Designed for gamers requiring strong 1440p and 4K performance at slightly lower costs than the RX 9000.

RX 6000 Series

Based on RDNA 2 architecture; this series introduced hardware-accelerated ray tracing and PCIe 4.0 support, marking a significant step up in gaming capabilities. Suitable for 1080p, 1440p, and some 4K gaming depending on SKU.

RX 5000 Series

The initial RDNA architecture; these cards improved performance and power efficiency significantly over previous GCN-based cards. Preferred for solid 1080p and 1440p gaming during their release period, now budget-friendly options for used markets.

RX 500 Series

Based on Polaris architecture, this series refreshed the RX 400 lineup with higher clocks and better efficiency. Geared towards entry-level and budget PC builds offering competent 1080p gaming but largely outdated today.

AMD Radeon RX GPU Model Number Breakdown

AMD GPU model numbers usually follow a format like RX 7900 XT, where each part of the number carries specific meaning:

  • First digit example 7 in 7900: Refers to the generation of the GPU.
    • 9 = 9000 Series (Latest RDNA 4 architecture)
    • 7 = 7000 Series (Previous generation RDNA 3)
    • 6 = 6000 Series (RDNA 2)
    • 5 = 5000 Series (First RDNA architecture)
    • 4 and below refer to older generations based on GCN or Polaris
  • Second digit example 9 in 7900: Represents the market segment or product tier, showing relative performance within that generation.
    • 9 = Enthusiast/flagship tier (top-end GPU)
    • 8 = High-end tier, powerful but slightly below flagship
    • 7 = Upper mid-range, strong gaming and performance
    • 6 = Mid-range segment, balanced performance and cost
    • 5 = Entry-level to mid-range, good for 1080p gaming
    • 4 and below = More budget or mobile-focused SKUs
  • Last two digits example 00 in 7900 or 70 in 7870: Indicate the specific SKU or model within the tier and generation. Higher numbers generally correspond to better features, higher clock speeds, or metal/board-level differences but not always strictly linear. This often differentiates between models with additional VRAM, better cooling, factory overclocking, or other optimizations.
  • Suffix letters: Indicate special features or power targets.
    • XT: Higher performance variant tuned for better clocks and speeds.
    • XTX: Flagship or extreme edition, usually with max clocks and full-chip unlocks.
    • M: Mobile versions for laptops.
    • SUPER or PLUS: Mid-cycle refreshes with improved specs or clockspeed bumps.
